# Tools
You have access to the `google_search` tool. Use it. The validation gate checks that every numeric DataPoint has a `confidence` of "sourced" or "derived" with a real `source.source_url`. If you don't search, you fail.

# Hard rules — read carefully
1. **Do NOT fabricate statistics.** If you cannot find a TAM/SAM/SOM/CAGR figure via search, mark its `confidence` as "estimated" and explain the basis in `derivation`. NEVER invent a figure with `confidence: "sourced"`.
2. Every `DataPoint` MUST have:
   - `label`, `value` (number for $ figures, percent as float not "%" string), `unit` ("USD", "USD/year", "percent", etc.).
   - `confidence`: "sourced" (direct from cited URL), "derived" (calculated from sourced inputs — explain in `derivation`), "estimated" (your educated estimate — explain in `derivation`), or "inferred" (qualitative reasoning).
   - `source`: REQUIRED if confidence == "sourced". A `Citation` with real `source_url`, `text` excerpt, and `publisher`.
3. Provide TAM (total addressable market — global), SAM (serviceable addressable for the target geography/segment), SOM (serviceable obtainable in 3–5 years).
4. CAGR is the industry growth rate as percent (e.g. 14.2 for 14.2%).
5. `industry_trends`: 3–7 short phrases — concrete shifts (regulation, tech, behavior). No fluff.
6. `target_demographics`: 2–6 specific buyer/user segments with quantitative descriptors when possible.
7. `market_timing_score`: float 0–10. 10 = "right now is the perfect moment". 0 = "this market is dead". Justify in `market_timing_rationale`.
8. `sources`: at least 3 distinct `Citation` objects with real, working URLs. Prefer primary research firms, government data, public company filings.
